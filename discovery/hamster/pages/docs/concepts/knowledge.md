# Knowledge — shared understanding across your tools

Most of what a team knows about its own product doesn't live in a spec. It lives in a PR from three months ago, a Linear ticket linked to a Slack thread, a Figma file shared by URL, a customer call where the real reason for a feature got mentioned in passing.

Every time someone picks up a new [Brief](https://tryhamster.com/docs/hamster-studio/briefs) — a teammate, a new joiner, an agent — the same questions come up: what do we already have, why does it work that way, what did we decide last quarter and why? When the answers are in reach, the work moves. When they're not, someone has to go find the person who remembers.

[Knowledge](https://tryhamster.com/docs/concepts/knowledge) is how Hamster keeps those answers in the room. The assistant your team uses is general-purpose; the English layer inside Hamster is yours. [Blueprints](https://tryhamster.com/docs/hamster-studio/blueprints) describe what's true about your product; [Methods](https://tryhamster.com/docs/hamster-studio/skills-methods) describe how your team works; the [Context Graph](https://tryhamster.com/docs/hamster-studio/context-graph) links it to your real systems. Hamster drafts most of it from what your team is already doing — chats, PRs, tickets, calls — and your team steers it.

Here, _shared understanding becomes infrastructure_: the next person on the work, teammate or agent, starts from what the team already knows instead of reconstructing it. That's what keeps the last 30% grounded in the team instead of improvised from a prompt.

## Knowledge runs both ways

Hamster Studio reads in both directions between English — [Goals](https://tryhamster.com/docs/hamster-studio/goals), [Initiatives](https://tryhamster.com/docs/hamster-studio/initiatives), [Briefs](https://tryhamster.com/docs/hamster-studio/briefs), [Blueprints](https://tryhamster.com/docs/hamster-studio/blueprints), [Methods](https://tryhamster.com/docs/hamster-studio/skills-methods) — and the concrete artefacts in your real systems: code, Linear tickets, GitHub PRs, Figma frames, Notion pages, call transcripts.

Take a [Brief](https://tryhamster.com/docs/hamster-studio/briefs) from chat to a [Plan](https://tryhamster.com/docs/hamster-studio/plans) to a PR, and Hamster carries the work end to end. Update a [Blueprint](https://tryhamster.com/docs/hamster-studio/blueprints), and the next [Brief](https://tryhamster.com/docs/hamster-studio/briefs) that draws on it starts from the new picture. Sync a codebase or a Notion dump, and Hamster updates the [Blueprint](https://tryhamster.com/docs/hamster-studio/blueprints) to reflect what's true; [Goal](https://tryhamster.com/docs/hamster-studio/goals) [Results](https://tryhamster.com/docs/hamster-studio/results) roll forward; [Methods](https://tryhamster.com/docs/hamster-studio/skills-methods) catch up with how work has actually been done.

That's why [Briefs](https://tryhamster.com/docs/hamster-studio/briefs), [Blueprints](https://tryhamster.com/docs/hamster-studio/blueprints), [Goals](https://tryhamster.com/docs/hamster-studio/goals), and [Initiatives](https://tryhamster.com/docs/hamster-studio/initiatives) aren't write-once documents. They're a living English representation of your product — drafted by Hamster from what your team is already doing, kept current as work ships, read by humans and agents through the same surface.

## Blueprints — what's true today

A [Blueprint](https://tryhamster.com/docs/hamster-studio/blueprints) is an English representation of something stable in your business: a product, a system, a team, a process. It captures what is _true today_ — not what you're changing, not how you do the work, just the current state.

When an AI agent (or a new engineer, or a new PM) picks up work, the same questions surface every time: what is this product, what does it do, what's the architecture, who uses it, what already exists. A [Blueprint](https://tryhamster.com/docs/hamster-studio/blueprints) is the answer to those questions, written once and read by everyone — agent and human — every time a [Brief](https://tryhamster.com/docs/hamster-studio/briefs) gets refined.

[Blueprints](https://tryhamster.com/docs/hamster-studio/blueprints) come in a few common shapes: product [Blueprints](https://tryhamster.com/docs/hamster-studio/blueprints) describe what something does and who uses it; system [Blueprints](https://tryhamster.com/docs/hamster-studio/blueprints) describe internal services and how they fit together; team [Blueprints](https://tryhamster.com/docs/hamster-studio/blueprints) describe how a team operates. The hard rule: a [Blueprint](https://tryhamster.com/docs/hamster-studio/blueprints) describes _state_, not change. Change lives in [Briefs](https://tryhamster.com/docs/hamster-studio/briefs).

You don't usually write a [Blueprint](https://tryhamster.com/docs/hamster-studio/blueprints) from scratch. Hamster generates a first draft from your [Context Graph](https://tryhamster.com/docs/hamster-studio/context-graph), your team shapes it to taste, and from then on the [Blueprint](https://tryhamster.com/docs/hamster-studio/blueprints) stays current as the [Context Graph](https://tryhamster.com/docs/hamster-studio/context-graph) notices code, tickets, and docs moving underneath it.

## Methods — how your team works

A [Method](https://tryhamster.com/docs/hamster-studio/skills-methods) is an instruction for how AI should help humans do a particular kind of work. It captures _the way your team operates_ — how a [Brief](https://tryhamster.com/docs/hamster-studio/briefs) for a billing change should be written, how a database migration should be tested, how a design-system change should be reviewed.

An assistant becomes useful when it knows how your team operates. [Methods](https://tryhamster.com/docs/hamster-studio/skills-methods) are how your conventions become part of every [Brief](https://tryhamster.com/docs/hamster-studio/briefs), every [Plan](https://tryhamster.com/docs/hamster-studio/plans), every [Delivery](https://tryhamster.com/docs/concepts/delivery) — drafted from how your team has actually been working, edited when you want a different default.

Hamster ships with a default — the Hamster [Method](https://tryhamster.com/docs/hamster-studio/skills-methods) — that covers the most common patterns: how to write a [Brief](https://tryhamster.com/docs/hamster-studio/briefs), how to [Plan](https://tryhamster.com/docs/hamster-studio/plans), how to deliver, how to handle PRs. New teams ship effectively with the default. When a convention emerges that the default doesn't cover (specific testing patterns, deploy procedures, design-system rules), you fork the Hamster [Method](https://tryhamster.com/docs/hamster-studio/skills-methods) into your workspace and add what's needed. Fork once at the workspace level; drift across per-team forks compounds.

## Context Graph — the connective tissue

The [Context Graph](https://tryhamster.com/docs/hamster-studio/context-graph) is the connected, summarised view of everything your team has already accumulated — your code, your tickets, your design files, your docs, your conversations — linked together so AI can read across all of it at once.

Most context in a software team doesn't live in a spec. It lives in a PR from three months ago, a Linear ticket linked to a Slack thread linked to a Loom, a Figma file shared by URL, a Notion page that was right two months ago, a customer call where the real reason for a feature got mentioned in passing. The Graph is how the AI sees all of it without anyone moving it.

The [Context Graph](https://tryhamster.com/docs/hamster-studio/context-graph) connects three layers: Hamster's own artefacts ([Goals](https://tryhamster.com/docs/hamster-studio/goals), [Initiatives](https://tryhamster.com/docs/hamster-studio/initiatives), [Briefs](https://tryhamster.com/docs/hamster-studio/briefs), [Blueprints](https://tryhamster.com/docs/hamster-studio/blueprints), [Methods](https://tryhamster.com/docs/hamster-studio/skills-methods)); external context summarised from [GitHub](https://tryhamster.com/docs/hamster-studio/connections/github), [Linear](https://tryhamster.com/docs/hamster-studio/connections/linear), [Slack](https://tryhamster.com/docs/hamster-studio/connections/slack), [Figma](https://tryhamster.com/docs/hamster-studio/connections/figma), [Notion](https://tryhamster.com/docs/hamster-studio/connections/notion), [Google Drive](https://tryhamster.com/docs/hamster-studio/connections/google-drive), and the [Meeting Agent](https://tryhamster.com/docs/hamster-studio/connections/meeting-agent); and the relationships between everything. The relationships are what makes the [Context Graph](https://tryhamster.com/docs/hamster-studio/context-graph) useful rather than just exhaustive.

The [Context Graph](https://tryhamster.com/docs/hamster-studio/context-graph) isn't tied to any specific tool. If your team uses Jira instead of Linear, swap the [Connection](https://tryhamster.com/docs/hamster-studio/connections). If you move off a tool entirely, your [Blueprints](https://tryhamster.com/docs/hamster-studio/blueprints), [Methods](https://tryhamster.com/docs/hamster-studio/skills-methods), and [Briefs](https://tryhamster.com/docs/hamster-studio/briefs) stay in Hamster — what migrates is the [Connection](https://tryhamster.com/docs/hamster-studio/connections), not what you've built.

## How Knowledge gets used

[Knowledge](https://tryhamster.com/docs/concepts/knowledge) stays available across every loop:

* When you start a [Brief](https://tryhamster.com/docs/hamster-studio/briefs), the relevant [Blueprints](https://tryhamster.com/docs/hamster-studio/blueprints) come along automatically.
* When AI ships a PR, it's already read your [Methods](https://tryhamster.com/docs/hamster-studio/skills-methods).
* When you ask a question in [Hamster Chat](https://tryhamster.com/docs/hamster-studio/chat), your [Context Graph](https://tryhamster.com/docs/hamster-studio/context-graph) is doing the lookup behind the answer.

When [Blueprints](https://tryhamster.com/docs/hamster-studio/blueprints) and [Methods](https://tryhamster.com/docs/hamster-studio/skills-methods) are in place, [Briefs](https://tryhamster.com/docs/hamster-studio/briefs) and PRs line up with how your team actually builds, and answers in [Hamster Chat](https://tryhamster.com/docs/hamster-studio/chat) stay grounded in your real systems through the [Context Graph](https://tryhamster.com/docs/hamster-studio/context-graph).

## Where to start

The [Context Graph](https://tryhamster.com/docs/hamster-studio/context-graph) is what populates [Knowledge](https://tryhamster.com/docs/concepts/knowledge). Connect the systems where work already happens — start with [GitHub](https://tryhamster.com/docs/hamster-studio/connections/github) and [Slack](https://tryhamster.com/docs/hamster-studio/connections/slack), then add Linear or Jira, Figma, Notion, Google Drive, and the [Meeting Agent](https://tryhamster.com/docs/hamster-studio/connections/meeting-agent) as your team needs them. Hamster drafts from that context, and your team steers what should become durable.
